### 1. Albert Einstein

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Title** | Distinguished Professor of Temporal Sciences |
| **Department** | Temporal Sciences |
| **Era/Campus** | 20th Century European Campus (Zurich Annex, 1905) |

**Courses Taught:**

- **TEMP 301 — Relativistic Chrononautics: Why Your Twin Is Younger Than You** — A rigorous exploration of time dilation, reference frames, and their practical consequences for academy field trips.
- **TEMP 415 — The Fabric of Spacetime: Knitting Reality Back Together** — Advanced seminar on general relativity as it pertains to temporal corridor stability.
- **TEMP 210 — Thought Experiments for the Reckless** — An introductory lab where students ride elevators, chase light beams, and argue with cats in boxes (guest lecturer: Schrodinger).

**Teaching Style:** Professor Einstein famously refuses to use slides, preferring to fill every chalkboard in the building with equations, then wander the halls barefoot asking students if they "feel" the answer yet. Exams are open-universe.

**Famous Quote (repurposed):**
> *"Imagination is more important than knowledge — especially when you've left your textbook in the 18th century."*

**Office Hours Note:** Office hours are held "whenever time permits, which, as I have proven, is relative." In practice, he is usually found in the faculty lounge playing violin and happy to talk between movements.

### 2. Hypatia of Alexandria

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Title** | Dean of Natural Sciences & Medicine |
| **Department** | Natural Sciences & Medicine |
| **Era/Campus** | Ancient Mediterranean Campus (Alexandria Library Wing, ~400 CE) |

**Courses Taught:**

- **NSCI 101 — Astronomy Before Telescopes: Navigating by Naked Eye and Pure Nerve** — Celestial mechanics, star mapping, and the lost art of looking up.
- **NSCI 250 — The Mathematics of Everything That Moves** — Applied mathematics covering conic sections, orbital paths, and why planets refuse to travel in straight lines.

**Teaching Style:** Dean Hypatia conducts lectures in the open courtyard regardless of weather, insisting that "a roof is just a ceiling on your curiosity." She is known for Socratic questioning so relentless that students have reportedly confessed to paradoxes they didn't commit.

**Famous Quote (repurposed):**
> *"Reserve your right to think, for even to think wrongly is better than not to think at all — though please do show your work on the final."*

**Office Hours Note:** Available sunrise to sunset in the Library Wing atrium. Warns students that she does not suffer fools, but gladly suffers the genuinely confused. Bring your own papyrus.

### 3. Leonardo da Vinci

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Title** | Chair of Innovation & Engineering / Adjunct Professor of Arts & Expression |
| **Department** | Innovation & Engineering (primary) / Arts & Expression (secondary) |
| **Era/Campus** | Renaissance Italian Campus (Florence Workshop, ~1500) |

**Courses Taught:**

- **ENGR 320 — Machines That Probably Should Not Fly (But Do)** — Aeronautics, mechanical engineering, and the fine line between genius and recklessness.
- **ARTS 275 — Anatomical Drawing for Time Travelers: Know What You're Made Of** — Human anatomy through observational sketching, dissection, and deeply uncomfortable eye contact with cadavers.
- **ENGR 410 — Reverse-Engineering the Future** — Students bring objects from later eras; da Vinci attempts to build them from 15th-century materials. Results vary wildly.

**Teaching Style:** Lectures are delivered in mirror-script on scrolls that unroll from the ceiling. Professor da Vinci teaches every class simultaneously — painting a fresco while explaining gear ratios while critiquing your posture. He has never once finished a syllabus on time.

**Famous Quote (repurposed):**
> *"Simplicity is the ultimate sophistication — which is why your semester project should be no more than seven hundred pages."*

**Office Hours Note:** Technically always available, as he sleeps in 20-minute intervals throughout the day. However, finding him requires checking the workshop, the anatomy lab, the bell tower, the river (he may be testing a submarine), and occasionally the kitchen.

### 4. Marie Curie

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Title** | Professor of Natural Sciences & Temporal Radiation Studies |
| **Department** | Natural Sciences & Medicine |
| **Era/Campus** | Early 20th Century European Campus (Paris Laboratory, ~1903) |

**Courses Taught:**

- **NSCI 340 — Glowing Reviews: Radioactivity and Its Discontents** — The physics and chemistry of radioactive decay, including temporal isotope half-lives unique to chrononaut exposure.
- **NSCI 445 — Laboratory Safety Across the Ages** — A mandatory seminar on how not to poison yourself, irradiate yourself, or accidentally catalyze a temporal rift. Based on extensive personal experience.

**Teaching Style:** Madame Curie's labs are famously rigorous. She keeps a notebook for every student and remembers every data point they have ever recorded. Her feedback is precise, her standards are merciless, and her praise — rare as radium — glows twice as bright when earned.

**Famous Quote (repurposed):**
> *"Nothing in life is to be feared, it is only to be understood — now put on your lead apron and stop complaining."*

**Office Hours Note:** Professor Curie holds office hours in her lab, which she never leaves. She asks that students not touch anything that is glowing, humming, or warm to the touch. Appointments run exactly on schedule. There is no "fashionably late" in radioactivity.

### 6. Frida Kahlo

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Title** | Professor of Arts & Expression |
| **Department** | Arts & Expression |
| **Era/Campus** | 20th Century Americas Campus (Casa Azul Studio, Mexico City, ~1940) |

**Courses Taught:**

- **ARTS 310 — Self-Portraiture as Temporal Autobiography** — Using personal imagery to document identity across timelines. Students produce a self-portrait series reflecting who they are in three different eras.
- **ARTS 330 — Pain, Color, and the Canvas That Fights Back** — An advanced studio course exploring suffering, resilience, and radical honesty through visual art.

**Teaching Style:** Professor Kahlo teaches from a throne of pillows surrounded by monkeys, parrots, and at least one hairless dog of uncertain temporal origin. Critiques are devastating, compassionate, and delivered with a directness that has made grown chrononauts weep. She insists every painting must "bleed a little truth."

**Famous Quote (repurposed):**
> *"I paint my own reality. The only thing I know is that I paint because I need to — and because this department has a 'publish or perish' policy."*

**Office Hours Note:** Available most afternoons at the Casa Azul campus. Students are welcome to join her for tea, but be warned: she will ask you deeply personal questions and then paint your answer whether you like it or not.

### 9. Ada Lovelace

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Title** | Associate Professor of Innovation & Engineering |
| **Department** | Innovation & Engineering |
| **Era/Campus** | 19th Century British Campus (London Salon, ~1843) |

**Courses Taught:**

- **ENGR 260 — Algorithms Before Electricity: Thinking Like a Machine So Machines Don't Have To** — Foundational computational logic, program design, and the poetic elegance of well-structured instructions.
- **ENGR 370 — Analytical Engines and Temporal Computation** — Designing mechanical and early-digital systems capable of processing chrono-data, including Babbage Engine compatibility modules.

**Teaching Style:** Professor Lovelace structures her courses like symphonies — each lecture builds on the last in movements of increasing complexity, culminating in a final project she calls "the crescendo." **Famous Quote (repurposed):**
> *"The Analytical Engine weaves algebraic patterns just as the Jacquard loom weaves flowers and leaves — and your code, currently, weaves neither. Revise."*

**Office Hours Note:** Available Wednesday afternoons in the London Salon. She requests that students come prepared with specific questions, not vague despair. "I can debug your logic, but not your existential crisis — for that, see the Philosophy department."

### 10. Marcus Aurelius

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Title** | Chair of Applied Philosophy & Ethics |
| **Department** | Applied Philosophy & Ethics |
| **Era/Campus** | Imperial Roman Campus (Rome, ~170 CE) |

**Courses Taught:**

- **PHIL 101 — Stoicism for Time Travelers: Accepting What You Cannot Change (Especially the Past)** — An introduction to Stoic ethics with practical application to the unique emotional hazards of temporal displacement.
- **PHIL 340 — Meditations on Power and Responsibility** — A seminar exploring the ethics of authority, drawing on case studies from Roman governance and the temptations inherent in time travel.
- **PHIL 490 — The Examined Temporal Life** — Capstone course. Students maintain a philosophical journal across three era-placements and defend their personal ethical framework before a faculty panel.

**Teaching Style:** Chairman Aurelius teaches while walking — through gardens, colonnades, and occasionally active construction sites. He speaks quietly and expects students to keep pace, both physically and intellectually. He never raises his voice. He never needs to. He grades journals, not exams, and reads every word.

**Famous Quote (repurposed):**
> *"Waste no more time arguing about what a good student should be. Be one."*

**Office Hours Note:** Available at dawn in the campus gardens. He walks; you walk with him. Conversations are confidential. He has heard confessions from emperors and chrononauts alike and judges neither. Bring comfortable shoes.

### 11. Murasaki Shikibu

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Title** | Professor of Arts & Expression |
| **Department** | Arts & Expression |
| **Era/Campus** | Heian Period Eastern Campus (Kyoto Imperial Court, ~1005 CE) |

**Courses Taught:**

- **ARTS 340 — Narrative Across Centuries: Writing Stories That Outlive Their Authors** — Long-form fiction, character development, and the structural techniques that make a story survive a thousand years.
- **ARTS 420 — The Literature of Longing: Emotion as Temporal Architecture** — Advanced seminar on how desire, loss, and memory function as narrative engines, with readings spanning every era on the Academy's calendar.

**Teaching Style:** Professor Murasaki teaches behind a silk screen, her voice carrying clearly while her expression remains a mystery. She believes great writing requires careful observation and emotional courage in equal measure. Workshops are conducted by exchanging anonymized manuscripts on folded paper, with feedback delivered as poetry. Students either love this or find it "profoundly disorienting," which she considers the same thing.

**Famous Quote (repurposed):**
> *"The truth is to be found in the fiction — which is why your personal essay reads false and your short story reads true. Reverse them."*

**Office Hours Note:** Professor Murasaki is available by written appointment only. Slide a note under the screen door of her study in the Kyoto Wing. She will respond within one day, or one century — whichever she finds more poetically appropriate.

### 12. Ibn al-Haytham (Alhazen)

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Title** | Professor of Natural Sciences & Temporal Optics |
| **Department** | Natural Sciences & Medicine |
| **Era/Campus** | Medieval Islamic Golden Age Campus (Cairo, ~1020 CE) |

**Courses Taught:**

- **NSCI 220 — Optics, Perception, and the Light Between Timelines** — How light behaves, how eyes deceive, and why temporal shimmer makes corridor exits look closer than they are.
- **NSCI 380 — The Scientific Method: A User's Manual for Every Era** — Experimental design, hypothesis testing, and evidence-based reasoning, taught as a universal toolkit that works whether you are in 1020 or 3020.

**Teaching Style:** Professor Ibn al-Haytham insists on experimental proof for everything. Claim the sky is blue? Prove it. Claim you did the reading? Prove it. He builds pinhole cameras, prisms, and optical benches in class and expects students to discover principles through observation before he names them. Lectures are structured as guided investigations, not monologues.

**Famous Quote (repurposed):**
> *"The duty of the man who investigates the writings of scientists is not to trust the authority of the textbook, but to subject it to experiment — yes, even my textbook."*

**Office Hours Note:** Available daily in the Cairo optics laboratory. Bring a question and a willingness to test your assumptions. He keeps a "Wall of Disproven Hypotheses" and considers getting posted on it a badge of honor. "Only those who test can fail. Only those who fail can learn."

### 13. Harriet Tubman

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Title** | Professor of Strategy & Leadership |
| **Department** | Strategy & Leadership |
| **Era/Campus** | 19th Century American Campus (Eastern Seaboard Corridor, ~1855) |

**Courses Taught:**

- **LEAD 280 — Navigation Under Impossible Conditions** — Wayfinding, covert logistics, and decision-making when every variable is hostile. Uses the Underground Railroad as primary case study and temporal corridors as the practicum.
- **LEAD 350 — Courage as Infrastructure: Building Networks That Survive** — How to create resilient systems of trust, communication, and mutual aid that function under surveillance and across eras.

**Teaching Style:** Professor Tubman does not lecture. She leads. Classes meet at unpredictable locations announced via coded messages left at designated drops around campus. She has never lost a student, and she never will.

**Famous Quote (repurposed):**
> *"I freed a thousand slaves. I could have freed a thousand more if only they knew they were enrolled — I mean, enslaved."*

**Office Hours Note:** "If you need me, follow the North Star. If you can't find the North Star, take my Intro to Navigation course. I don't repeat directions."

### 14. Pythagoras

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Title** | Professor of Temporal Sciences / Adjunct in Applied Philosophy |
| **Department** | Temporal Sciences (primary) / Applied Philosophy & Ethics (secondary) |
| **Era/Campus** | Ancient Mediterranean Campus (Croton, Southern Italy, ~520 BCE) |

**Courses Taught:**

- **TEMP 110 — The Harmony of Time: Mathematical Foundations of Temporal Mechanics** — Number theory, geometric proof, and the surprising relationship between musical intervals and temporal wavelengths.
- **PHIL 215 — Numbers, Souls, and the Shape of the Universe** — A cross-listed philosophy course exploring Pythagorean metaphysics and the idea that reality is, at its core, mathematical.

**Teaching Style:** Professor Pythagoras begins every class with a musical tuning exercise, insisting students cannot think clearly until they are "in harmony." Lectures involve geometric proofs drawn in sand, chanted theorems, and an absolute prohibition on beans in the classroom (he will not explain why, and the faculty handbook says we cannot make him). Students report a cult-like devotion to his courses that they find "alarming but academically productive."

**Famous Quote (repurposed):**
> *"There is geometry in the humming of the strings, there is music in the spacing of the spheres — and there will be consequences in the spacing of your citations."*

**Office Hours Note:** Available at his commune in the Croton Wing, but visitors must first answer a mathematical riddle at the door. Wrong answers result in a mandatory five-minute silent contemplation before a second attempt is granted.

### 15. Mary Wollstonecraft

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Title** | Associate Professor of Applied Philosophy & Ethics |
| **Department** | Applied Philosophy & Ethics |
| **Era/Campus** | 18th Century European Campus (London, ~1792) |

**Courses Taught:**

- **PHIL 300 — A Vindication of the Rights of Time Travelers** — Political philosophy, human rights theory, and the ethical obligations of those who can witness injustice across centuries and choose whether to act.
- **PHIL 335 — Education as Revolution: Pedagogy Across the Ages** — How education systems shape (and are shaped by) power structures, with comparative case studies from every era the Academy touches.

**Teaching Style:** Professor Wollstonecraft runs her seminars as structured debates with real stakes — losing a round means you must write the opposing argument's strongest version. She is fiercely egalitarian in her classroom, famously interrupting a visiting emperor mid-sentence to let a first-year student finish their point. Papers are graded on the courage of the argument, not just its polish.

**Famous Quote (repurposed):**
> *"I do not wish women to have power over men, but over themselves — and over their own thesis deadlines, which several of you are currently failing to manage."*

**Office Hours Note:** Open-door policy, literally — she removed her office door in the first week and refuses to have it reinstalled. "Barriers to knowledge are the first walls a philosopher must tear down. Also, the doorknob was broken and Facilities hasn't responded to my request from 1792."

### 16. Srinivasa Ramanujan

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Title** | Associate Professor of Temporal Sciences |
| **Department** | Temporal Sciences |
| **Era/Campus** | Early 20th Century British Campus (Cambridge, ~1916) |

**Courses Taught:**

- **TEMP 450 — Infinite Series and Temporal Convergence** — How infinite mathematical series model the behavior of branching timelines, and why some infinities are more useful than others.
- **TEMP 320 — Intuition and Proof: Mathematical Discovery Beyond Method** — A seminar on non-linear problem solving, pattern recognition, and the role of intuition in temporal mathematics.

**Teaching Style:** Professor Ramanujan fills notebooks at a speed that terrifies even the note-taking enchantments. He often presents results first and proofs later — sometimes much later — insisting that "the goddess told me" is a valid first step in mathematical discovery (the department requires a formal proof by the second step). He is extraordinarily patient with struggling students and astonishingly impatient with those who call something "impossible."

**Famous Quote (repurposed):**
> *"An equation means nothing to me unless it expresses a thought of God — or at minimum earns partial credit on the exam."*

**Office Hours Note:** Found most reliably in the Cambridge dining hall, scribbling on napkins. He welcomes all visitors but warns that conversations tend to "drift into the infinite" and you may lose track of several hours. Bring snacks; he forgets to eat.

## Inter-Departmental Notes

- **Da Vinci and Lovelace** co-teach an annual workshop called "The Machine Imagination" where students design devices that are half-art, half-engineering, and fully impractical.
- **Einstein and Ramanujan** have a standing Tuesday argument in the faculty lounge about whether intuition or rigor matters more. Neither has won. Both claim victory.
- **Cleopatra and Sun Tzu** jointly advise the Academy's Model United Timelines student organization and have turned it into the most competitive extracurricular on campus.
- **Kahlo and Murasaki Shikibu** run a cross-era salon called "The Chrysanthemum and the Cactus" where students share creative work in any medium. Attendance is technically optional but socially mandatory.
- **Tubman and Aurelius** have a surprising friendship rooted in mutual respect for quiet endurance. They co-teach a one-week intensive each January called "Walking Forward: Resilience Across Millennia."
- **Wollstonecraft and Hypatia** are co-authoring a paper titled "Two Thousand Years of Being Interrupted: A Longitudinal Study" that the Philosophy department is eagerly awaiting.
- **Ibn al-Haytham and Curie** share a laboratory safety committee seat and have co-authored the Academy's 400-page Lab Safety Manual, subtitle: "We Learned This the Hard Way So You Don't Have To."
